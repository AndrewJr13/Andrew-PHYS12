<br> <br> <head>Class 3: Electronics and Tools</head> For week 3, I had a lot of ideas. My initial thought was to make the locking mechanism for my final project but I came to the conclusion that without something like an Arduino to change the direction that the motor would turn, the project would be ultimatley useless and overly complicated. My second thought was the one that came to life. I decided to make a windmill. This idea formulated very well in my mind so I did not have to use a ton of scetches. I used a digital caliper to make sure I wasn't going to make it too big or too small. Next, I went to fusion 360 and started with the supports of the windmill. I decided to have four main legs on the windmill and some connecting pieces that would go around all of the legs. I went into Fusion and started to design. These were my projections for the supports: ![](./supports.png) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; ![](./supports1.png) I planned to glue the supports in the first picture together to form the legs. This turned out tobe pretty sucessful. Next, I moved on to the part that would attach to the motor. I decided to take the dimensions of the shaft on the motor and make a square inscribed in a circle. The square would fit right on the shaft and I could attach my propellers to the circle. Now, for the actual attaching of the propellers, I had to do this at home since I was not able to finish it in the lab. I ran into a large roadblock that the glue I was using would not bind the circular part to the propellers. So I had to improvise. I actually ended up using rolled up pieces of paper and putting glue on them. I then inserted the paper into the carboard on the propeller and then into the cardboard of the circular part. As for the motor itself, I used a soldering iron to make sure the wires were on well. I was actually decently proud of my solders as it was only my 3rd time ever using a soldering iron. I also ended up having to get some more wire and bind it to the original wire since the original wire wouldn't reach the power source from all the way up on the windmill. The binds I made held very well and kept good conductivity. I will include a picture of those as well. The following pictures are of the circular part, the propellers, the final product of the motor part, and my solders on the motor: ![](./circle22.png) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; ![](./wings1.png) ![](./paper.png) ![](./solder.png) Finally, to assemble the final product, I took my parts home and used some glue to put it together in my dorm. The final product turned out a little more dissapointing than I would've wanted it to be. I feel like the idea was there but the looks of the final product were not too great. I feel like this was a good idea, some things just didn't turn out right. First of all, when I first used the laser cutter, the cuts did not come out good. Most of the cuts didn't make it through the cardboard and the following cuts I made were not much better. I finally was able to get good cuts but by that time I didn't have much time left. So even though I did finish this project in time, there were things I think that I could've improved on the make the final product better than it turned out. The motor spins the propellers fine and the solering part turned out well but just the look of the sculpture was a little less than I wanted it to be.
